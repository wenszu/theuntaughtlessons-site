Phase 1 · Workbook A

Think Clearly

Before you can communicate clearly, you need to think clearly. This workbook builds the core habit: structure first, words second. You'll learn to separate signal from noise, group ideas without overlap, and lead with the bottom line.

Your situation

MA is a strategy and marketing consultancy based in Tokyo. Its clients are mid-size firms navigating inflection points — expansion, repositioning, change.

Aiko Mori is the founder and managing director. Precise, demanding, and fast. She trusts people who can structure their thinking — and she notices immediately when they can't.

You are the new Chief of Staff. It's your first week. Your job is to make Aiko faster, not slower. That starts with how information reaches her.

01

Manager's messy top-of-mind

Not started ›
What you're practising

A BSP (Bottom-line Supporting Point) is a short, factual statement — one idea per line, no explanation attached. The discipline is compression: say the fact, not the story around it.

MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) means your categories don't overlap and nothing is left out. It's the most useful grouping discipline in structured communication.

This exercise: take a disorganised status update and restructure it so Aiko can read it in under 60 seconds before a board briefing.

1
Read the update

The Head of Strategy sent you a voice memo transcript at 7:40am. Aiko's board briefing starts at 9:00am. Read it carefully — then work through the steps below.

Voice memo — Head of Strategy, 07:38

"So the Tokyo team flagged that the Olympic project timeline is slipping — they said maybe two weeks but I think it's more like four. Also we haven't confirmed the venue for the client summit yet, the hotel we wanted is now saying there's a booking conflict, so we need to get back to them by Friday or we lose the date. The Tanaka account renewal is coming up end of month — revenue's fine, I think around ¥3.2M, so that's good. Design team is stretched — Hana is out sick this week and the Sato deck still isn't done. The new hire starts Monday. Budget-wise we're okay this quarter but I noticed our production spend for the Olympic project is running about 15% over estimate."

2
Add your BSPs

Write each key fact from the update as a short, clear statement — one per line. No explanation. Just the fact. Aim for 6–8 BSPs.

3
Group into three MECE buckets

Name three non-overlapping categories. Assign each BSP to exactly one. Start with a one-sentence bottom line before the buckets.

4
Compare your answer

When you're done with your version, reveal the sample answer below. Don't look before you've made a real attempt.

Sample answer
Bottom line
Three categories of risk require decisions before end of week.
Structured update
Bucket 1 — Delivery Risk • Olympic project: tracking 4 weeks behind schedule (not 2)
• Sato deck incomplete; Hana absent this week Bucket 2 — Client & Revenue Risk • Client summit venue unconfirmed — hotel deadline: Friday
• Tanaka renewal (¥3.2M) due end of month; status: on track Bucket 3 — Resource & Budget Risk • Olympic project production spend: +15% over estimate
• New hire starts Monday; design team capacity currently strained
Why this works

The bottom line orients the reader before they touch a single detail — they know what kind of update this is. The three MECE buckets eliminate overlap: each fact has exactly one home. A board member can scan this in 20 seconds and know precisely what needs a decision.

Common mistake

Grouping by project rather than by risk type. It's tempting to put everything Olympic-related in one bucket. But that conflates a delivery issue, a budget issue, and a resource issue. MECE requires that the grouping principle applies consistently — here, risk type is the principle.

Improve with AI
AI prompt

Why use AI here: AI can stress-test your structure faster than re-reading it yourself — useful for spotting overlap you've stopped noticing, and for sharpening a bottom line that feels close but isn't quite right.

Suggest one way to make the bottom line sharper Be direct. One short paragraph per point.

What to expect: Specific feedback on each bucket, flagged overlaps or missing items, and a suggested alternative bottom line. You're not looking for praise — you're looking for the gap you missed.

Before you paste: which grouping decision was hardest? Was there a fact that felt like it could belong in two buckets?

6
Reflect

What did you notice? What was harder than you expected? Write a sentence or two — this is for you, not for evaluation.
