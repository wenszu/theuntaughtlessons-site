Delivery practice

Make your message feel inevitable.

Your job is to take a strong recommendation and make it land when spoken. Not louder. Not more dramatic. Clearer, calmer, better paced, and easier to follow when the room is watching you.

Exercise brief

15 minutes Use The Playback to upload a recording, review feedback using the rubric, identify one or two improvements, and iterate on your script and delivery. When you finish, note the one delivery habit you will change first.

Your recording script

Put this where you can read it while recording. Aim for calm authority: short pauses, steady pace, and no rush through the turns.

The Olympics is one of the few remaining promises the world still recognizes: that excellence can be earned, witnessed, and remembered—by anyone, from anywhere.

That is why this matters.

And that is why we should be honest.

The problem is gravity — it was never awareness, popularity, being seen.

The world used to gather around the Games because there was no substitute. Now there are infinite stages, infinite feeds, infinite noise. Shared moments do not happen by default. Meaning is being compressed into clips. Trust is more fragile. And when commercial signals get louder than human stakes, sacredness starts to feel rented.

There is no nobility in working to make the idea of the Olympics heard. As it is, it is loud and deafening. Our duty is to make the Olympics inevitable again.

We believe that happens through three moves.

First: Ritual rebuilt—design shared moments the world can actually enter together, not “catch up on.”

Second: Meaning made legible—athlete-led story arcs that restore stakes, context, and consequence over time.

Third: One coherent spine—a modern thesis, a disciplined tone, and a clarity layer that tells a casual viewer, every day: what matters, why it matters, and who to care about—without losing prestige.

We will respect the cultural limitations. Neutrality is non-negotiable. Global sensitivity is mandatory. Sponsorship is real. Rights are real. We will not be naive. We will be precise.

In the next sixty to ninety days, we will propose one pilot that proves lift—not in buzz, but in gravity: live return, story retention, and trust.

The Olympics should once again feel like it is supposed to be: an open stage where human limits become history. Thank you, everyone, and let the games begin!

Record and upload

  1. Set up your phone. Open your camera in portrait video mode. Keep the phone vertical, prop it at eye level, and make sure your face and shoulders are visible.
  2. Put the script near the lens. Keep the script close enough to read without looking far away from the camera.
  3. Record one complete take. Use the timer if helpful. Pause between sections and keep the final line clean.
  4. Check sound and framing. Play the first few seconds. If the audio is muddy or the framing distracts, rerecord once.
  5. Open The Playback. Use the button below, then upload the video or audio file. If needed, move it from phone to laptop with AirDrop or Google Drive.
  6. Use the feedback for one better take. Ask The Playback what to fix first, then rerecord with that one change in mind.
