What is the Orchestrator?

Every advisory board needs someone whose job is not to add another opinion to the pile. Their job is to listen to all of it and then make a call. Not find the middle ground between everyone, not summarize what the room said, but actually decide what the right move is and say it clearly.

That is what the Orchestrator does. They sit above the debate, let the advisors do their work, and when the session is over, they are the one who tells you what to prioritize and what to do next.

There are two ways to think about who that person should be.

The first is to make the Orchestrator a version of yourself. You choose someone whose decision-making instincts, values, and way of weighing tradeoffs are close to yours. The reason this works is not because you want agreement. Your advisors will still push back hard. But at the end of the session, you want a final verdict that reflects how you would actually move in real life, not just what the loudest voice in the room concluded. This approach is especially useful when the decision ultimately belongs to you and you want to pressure-test your own thinking before you commit.

The second approach is to ask a completely different question: who is the most qualified person in the world to make this specific call, regardless of whether they think like you? If you are working through a consumer hardware product, you might put Steve Jobs in the Orchestrator seat. Your advisors still represent finance, legal, operations, the end user, and they each bring their own challenge. But when all of that input has been heard, Jobs is the one who weighs it through the lens of what makes a product genuinely great. His final verdict carries the authority of someone who has navigated exactly this kind of tension before and knows what actually matters.

Neither approach is better than the other. The question worth asking yourself before you choose is whether you need clarity on what you would do, or whether you need the best possible decision for this particular problem regardless of whose instincts it reflects.

One thing stays consistent no matter who you choose. The Orchestrator does not participate in the advisory session itself. They observe and let each voice make its case without interruption. Their role only begins when everyone else has finished, and when they speak, they are not recapping the conversation but closing it with a direction.
